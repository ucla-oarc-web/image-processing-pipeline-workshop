These open spaces acted as natural barriers, preventing the fire from spreading. Additionally, homes that survived tended to have more space between them and their neighbors, reducing the chance of fire jumping from one structure to another. The slope of the land and wind conditions, similar to those observed in the Tubbs and Camp Fires, likely contributed to the rapid spread through densely packed areas.

Many of these homes were situated in clusters with little to no vegetation management, providing ample fuel for the fire. The materials used in the construction of these homes, potentially more flammable roofing or siding, could also have played a role in their destruction, as seen in the Hawaii Wildfires.

**Recommendations for Rebuilding:**

To avoid similar outcomes in future fires, the neighborhood should consider increasing the spacing between homes to create natural firebreaks. Incorporating more fire-resistant materials in construction, such as Class A roofing and siding, will also help. Vegetation around homes should be carefully managed to reduce fuel loads. If these measures are taken, the probability of a similar outcome in a future fire would be significantly reduced, potentially lowering the risk by 60-70%.
