**Conclusions:**

Based on the provided images and historical fire data, homes outlined in green likely survived due to increased defensible space around structures, the use of fire-resistant building materials, and their positioning either upwind or on slopes away from the fire's origin. These factors collectively reduce a home's vulnerability by creating barriers to fire spread and using materials less likely to ignite.

Conversely, homes outlined in red probably burned because they were surrounded by dense vegetation, constructed with older, more flammable materials, and situated closely together. These conditions facilitate rapid fire spread, as seen in the Tubbs and Camp Fires, where similar factors led to extensive damage. The national weather service data indicating high winds on the fire day would exacerbate these conditions, pushing flames quickly from one structure to another.

The probability of similar outcomes in future fires can be significantly reduced if the neighborhood adopts fire-safe construction practices. This includes using fire-resistant materials for roofing and siding, maintaining clear defensible space around homes, and managing vegetation to prevent it from acting as fuel. Historical data from the Hawaii Wildfires supports these measures, showing that well-prepared homes are more likely to survive.

**Confidence Level: 8**

**Recommendations for Rebuilding:**

To avoid similar outcomes, the neighborhood should prioritize rebuilding with fire-resistant materials such as concrete, brick, and metal roofs. Increasing the spacing between homes and maintaining a 30-foot defensible space around each structure can help prevent fire from easily jumping between buildings. Vegetation should be regularly managed, removing dead plants and keeping trees pruned. Implementing these measures would likely reduce the probability of similar fire outcomes to less than 30% in future incidents, based on effective strategies observed in past wildfire analyses.
