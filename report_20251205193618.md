**Conclusions:**

Based on the provided photos and historical data from similar fires, the homes outlined in green likely survived due to three main factors. First, these homes are situated near significant firebreaks, such as the large open areas around the water tanks, which can slow or stop a fire's progress. Second, these surviving homes generally had more space around them, reducing the chance of fire jumping from one structure to another. Third, the positioning of these homes might have provided some protection from the wind direction on the day of the fire, similar to patterns observed in the Tubbs and Camp Fires.

The homes outlined in red burned primarily for three reasons. They were densely packed, allowing the fire to easily spread from house to house, a phenomenon well-documented in the Hawaii Wildfires. Additionally, many of these homes were located in the fire's direct path, with nothing to impede its progress. Lastly, national weather service data indicating strong winds on the day of the fire would have fanned the flames, making it harder to control and more likely to engulf closely situated structures.

**Recommendations for Rebuilding:**

To avoid similar outcomes in future fires, the neighborhood should consider increasing the spacing between homes to reduce the risk of fire spread. Creating and maintaining defensible spaces, such as the open areas around the water tanks, can act as effective firebreaks. Using fire-resistant building materials for roofing and siding can also help. Following these guidelines would significantly lower the probability of a similar outcome in future fires, making the community safer and more resilient.
